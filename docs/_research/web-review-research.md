# Review Clauses and Review Mechanisms in UN Mandates

**Research compiled:** 12 May 2026
**Purpose:** Source material for `/docs/review-mechanisms.md`, supporting PPB 2026/27 mandate analysis work.

This document covers two angles in parallel:

1. **Mandate Implementation Review (MIR)** — the formal exercises (2005-06, UN80 2025-26) to review the stock of UN mandates as a whole.
2. **Review mechanisms elsewhere in UN practice** — the many other places "review" appears as a standard structural element of mandates (peacekeeping renewals, sanctions, QCPR, UPR, treaty bodies, HLPF, conference reviews, strategic plan MTRs, biennialization, etc.).

The language patterns near the end (Section 11) are the most directly actionable for downstream NLP/grep work.

---

## Part 1 — Mandate Implementation Review

### 1.1 The 2005-2006 exercise

The original mandate review exercise was launched by paragraphs 163(b) and 164 of the **2005 World Summit Outcome (A/RES/60/1)**, in which Heads of State and Government requested the Secretary-General and called on the General Assembly to "review all mandates older than five years originating from resolutions of the General Assembly and other organs."

Implementing this, the Secretary-General issued the seminal report:

> **A/60/733 — "Mandating and delivering: analysis and recommendations to facilitate the review of mandates"** (30 March 2006)

A/60/733 identified four headline problems:

1. Burdensome reporting requirements;
2. Overlap between and within organs;
3. An unwieldy and duplicative architecture for implementation;
4. The gap between what was intended and actual implementation.

The Secretariat put forward **~7,000 mandates older than five years** for review (rising to **~9,000** if mandates approved within the previous five years were included).

The corresponding GA resolution — **A/RES/60/257** (8 May 2006) on Programme Planning — sat alongside the political-track resolution on the mandate review exercise itself. A dedicated **Ad Hoc Working Group on Mandate Review** was established. Sister documents from the period include the Secretary-General's accompanying briefings to the General Assembly emphasizing that "mandate review must ensure that organizations become greater than the sum of [their] parts."

**Why it failed (consensus view across analyses):**
- Divergence on scope: no agreement on which mandates would be reviewed.
- North–South mistrust: G77 feared the exercise was a US/EU/Japan vehicle to cut the UN budget and gut development mandates; the South argued it belonged in the Fifth (budget) Committee.
- Methodological infeasibility: the difficulty of costing individual mandates.
- The exercise degenerated into what CIC's 2025 analysis later called a "tedious and draining bookkeeping exercise" focused on the mandates database, ultimately abandoned through exhaustion.
- Self-interest: many delegations had pet mandates they did not want examined.
- Disconnect between mandate cycle and budget cycle; ambiguity over which Secretariat entity implemented which mandate.

### 1.2 The UN80 Mandate Implementation Review (2025-26)

Launched by SG António Guterres in **March 2025** as part of the UN's 80th-anniversary reform package. UN80 has three workstreams:

1. **Efficiency and Improvements** (structural changes, programme realignments)
2. **Mandate Implementation Review (MIR)** — Workstream 2
3. **Structural and Programmatic Realignments** ("Shifting Paradigms: United to Deliver", September 2025)

Coordinated by an internal Task Force led by USG Guy Ryder; a principal-level Steering Committee chaired by the Secretary-General oversees six working groups (SDG acceleration; peace and security; international financial architecture; digital technologies; UN governance; youth).

#### Key documents and figures

- **SG's MIR Report (July/September 2025)** — identified **more than 40,000 resolutions, decisions, and presidential statements** issued by the GA, SC, and ECOSOC since 1946.
- The Secretariat alone has **more than 3,600 unique mandates**.
- In 2024, the UN serviced **~27,000 formal meetings** and produced **>1,100 mandated reports** at a cost **exceeding USD 360 million**, ~10% of the regular budget.
- **~86% of active mandates lack sunset clauses or termination reviews.**
- **Over 30% of GA topics from 1990** were still on the agenda in 2024.
- More than half of GA resolutions are adopted annually with **>80% of the text repeated** from prior years.
- SC resolutions are now **~3× longer than 30 years ago.**
- **Over 15% of new GA mandates in 2024 included no new funding** ("zero-budget mandates" have quadrupled since 2000).
- **~85% of voluntary contributions are earmarked**; pooled funding <10%.
- ~2/3 of UN reports in 2024 had <2,000 downloads; ~20% of reports <1,000 downloads.

#### Methodology

Unlike the 2006 exercise, the 2025 MIR adopts a **systemic, lifecycle-based lens** rather than reviewing mandates one by one. Co-chaired implementation track: **New Zealand and Jamaica** ambassadors lead the **Informal Ad Hoc Working Group on the Mandate Implementation Review**, established by GA **Decision 79/571**. Member-state primacy reaffirmed by **GA resolution 79/318**.

#### The landmark resolution (March 2026)

On **31 March 2026**, the GA adopted (168-4-0) the resolution **"Mandate Creation, Implementation and Review for an Efficient and Effective United Nations"** (draft circulated 23 March 2026). Key provisions:

- Establishes principles for the **mandate lifecycle** (creation → implementation → review).
- When creating mandates, States are required to clarify scope and objectives, and to include **implementation roadmaps, timelines, review clauses and, as appropriate, sunset clauses**.
- Establishes a formal **Ad Hoc Working Group on Mandate Implementation Review** open to all Member States.
- Mandates a **registry** improving visibility of existing mandates across the UN system.
- Member States to develop **objective criteria** for mandate **"renewal, adaptation, merger, replacement or retirement"** (the five-verb formula).
- Working Group must develop modalities by **August 2026**.
- Review of **inactive, duplicative, or fully implemented mandates** by **end of December 2026**.
- Final Working Group report by **30 April 2027**.

#### Exclusions from review

- **Foundational mandates** (establishing universal human rights norms);
- **Structural mandates** (governing standing institutions like the Human Rights Council);
- Mandates related to **active political situations affecting international peace and security**.

#### Relationship to PPB 2026/27

The MIR feeds directly into the **Fifth Committee** programme-budget process. The Fifth Committee approved a **USD 3.45 billion** budget for 2026, reflecting:
- ~**15.1% reduction** in resources vs. 2025 appropriation ($577 million cut).
- **18.8% reduction in staffing** (revised staffing table of 11,594 posts, down 2,681).
- The "Revised estimates UN80" line item linked the budget directly to mandate consolidation.

The UN now budgets **annually** (since the 2019 transition from biennial budgets), though many specialized agencies (e.g., UNFCCC) retain biennium 2026-27 structures. Programme planning is governed by the **Regulations and Rules Governing Programme Planning, the Programme Aspects of the Budget, the Monitoring of Implementation and the Methods of Evaluation (PPBME)** — ST/SGB/2018/3. **GA resolution 58/269 (2003)** introduced the strategic-framework concept replacing the four-year medium-term plan. The **Committee for Programme and Coordination (CPC)** — established by ECOSOC resolution 920 (XXXIV) (1962) — is the primary subsidiary organ reviewing the programmatic aspects of the strategic framework; GA resolution **62/224** endorsed the strategic framework as the principal policy directive from biennium 2010-2011.

## Part 2 — Review Mechanisms Elsewhere in UN Mandate Practice

### 2.1 Security Council peacekeeping mandate renewals

Peacekeeping/political mission mandates are typically issued on **6-month or 12-month cycles**, with extensions adopted by Security Council resolution. The Secretariat publishes a consolidated reference document, *Security Council Reporting and Mandate Cycles*, listing each mission's cycle.

**Examples:**

| Mission | Cycle | Most recent action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| MONUSCO (DRC) | **12 months** | S/RES/2808 (2025), extended to 20 Dec 2026 |
| UNFICYP (Cyprus) | **6 months** | Standard 6-month renewals; Council "ready to review implementation after six months" |
| UNDOF (Golan) | **6 months** | Standard 6-month renewals |
| UNIFIL (Lebanon) | **~12 months** (debated) | S/RES/2790 (28 Aug 2025) — final extension to 31 Dec 2026 |
| MINURSO (W. Sahara) | **12 months** (April) | Annual April renewals; contested since 2017 |
| UNMISS (S. Sudan) | **12 months** | Annual |

**Drafting actors:** "Penholders" (typically P3 members) circulate draft renewal resolutions ~2 weeks before expiry, holding multiple rounds of negotiations.

**Strategic / independent reviews:** SC may request the SG to commission an **independent strategic review** of a mission (e.g., MONUSCO 2019 — S/2019/842 by Youssef Mahmoud; UNFICYP review by Wolfgang Weisbrod-Weber). These typically feed mandate-renewal cycles.

**"Christmas tree" critique:** missions burdened with excessive tasks — SC reform conversation increasingly emphasizes **prioritization and sequencing** of mandated tasks.

**"Within existing resources":** widespread SC drafting practice of avoiding budgetary implications.

### 2.2 Sanctions regime reviews

Sanctions committees and their **monitoring teams / panels of experts** are renewed on **12-month cycles** (with some variation). Each renewal resolution typically:

- Extends the sanctions measures (assets freeze, travel ban, arms embargo) for 12 months;
- Renews the **Panel of Experts / Monitoring Team** for ~13 months (one month past sanctions expiry to allow final reporting);
- Mandates an interim and final written report.

**Examples:**
- **1267/1989/2253 ISIL & Al-Qaida Monitoring Team** — renewed annually (most recently S/RES/2816 (2026) — 12-month extension).
- **1988 Taliban Monitoring Team** — annual.
- **Al-Shabaab / Somalia Panel of Experts** — S/RES/2806 (2025), to 31 Dec 2026.
- **Yemen** — S/RES/2758 (2024), to 15 Nov 2025.
- **Sudan** — S/RES/2725 (2024), to 12 Mar 2025.
- **DPRK Panel of Experts** — historically renewed annually since 2010; **terminated in April 2024** when Russia vetoed the renewal — illustrates how mandates can lapse via Council deadlock rather than affirmative termination.

**Standard language:** "decides to extend the mandate of the Panel of Experts until [date]"; "expresses its intent to review the measures … and to consider further action as appropriate"; "requests the Panel to provide a midterm update no later than [date] and a final report no later than 30 days prior to the expiration of its mandate."

### 2.3 Quadrennial Comprehensive Policy Review (QCPR)

The **QCPR** is the GA's primary policy instrument for UN operational activities for development.

- Established cycle: every **four years**, with annual implementation reports.
- **2024 QCPR:** **A/RES/79/226** (19 December 2024), 54th plenary session, guiding UNDS until 2028.
- Implementation reviewed annually via the **ECOSOC Operational Activities for Development Segment (OAS)**.
- Funds and programmes (UNDP, UNICEF, UNFPA, UN Women, WFP) align their **Strategic Plans** with the QCPR cycle.

### 2.4 Strategic plan mid-term reviews (UN funds & programmes)

Each fund/programme has a four-year Strategic Plan with a **mid-term review (MTR)** at the two-year mark.

- **UNDP Strategic Plan 2022-2025** — cumulative review (DP/2025); successor **2026-2029** (DP/2025/22).
- **UNICEF Strategic Plan 2022-2025** — MTR (E/ICEF/2024 series); successor **2026-2029** (UNICEF/2025/15).
- MTRs cover programmatic content **and the integrated budget**.
- Standard cadence: alignment with the QCPR cycle.

### 2.5 Universal Periodic Review (UPR)

- Created by **GA resolution 60/251** (2006), establishing the Human Rights Council.
- **4.5-year cycle**; each Member State reviewed once per cycle by the **UPR Working Group**.
- Three two-week WG sessions per year (Jan/Feb, Apr/May, Oct/Nov); 14 countries per session.
- Average **~200 recommendations** per State per cycle.
- Mid-cycle voluntary follow-up reports encouraged.

### 2.6 Human rights treaty body periodic reviews

- Each treaty body reviews each State Party on a **periodic cycle**.
- Treaty texts originally specified ~**4-5 year** periodicity; in practice slipped to ~8 years.
- **2019:** Human Rights Committee (CCPR) adopted a **predictable 8-year review cycle** — the first treaty body to do so.
- **2022:** all treaty bodies agreed to a predictable schedule.
- Process: List of Issues (LOI/LOIPR) → State report → constructive dialogue → Concluding Observations → follow-up cycle.

### 2.7 Human Rights Council Special Procedures

- **Thematic mandates:** **3-year** initial term, renewable once for another 3 years (6 years total typical).
- **Country mandates:** **1-year** terms, renewable annually.
- Periodic comprehensive review of the Council itself: HRC review took place after its first five years per GA 60/251.

### 2.8 High-Level Political Forum on Sustainable Development (HLPF)

- Annual under **ECOSOC** auspices (July, 8 days); quadrennial under **GA** (Heads of State).
- Member States present **Voluntary National Reviews (VNRs)** — 37 VNRs in 2025 (10 second-time, 23 third-time, 4 fourth-time).
- HLPF conducts **in-depth thematic reviews** of selected SDGs each year (2025: SDGs 3, 5, 8, 14, 17).
- 2025 theme: "Advancing sustainable, inclusive, science- and evidence-based solutions for the 2030 Agenda".
- Convenes under ECOSOC: 14-23 July 2025.
- Established by GA resolution **67/290** (2013); refined by **A/RES/70/299**.

### 2.9 Conference outcome reviews ("+5", "+10", "+20", "+30")

A long-standing practice: major conference outcomes are reviewed at fixed intervals.

- **Beijing+30 (CSW69, March 2025):** Review and Appraisal of the Beijing Declaration and Platform for Action — SG report **E/CN.6/2025/3/Rev.1**. Outcome organized around "6+1" priority areas (digital gender gap, women in economic development, ending VAWG, etc.).
- **ICPD+30 (2024):** UNFPA-led regional reviews of the 1994 ICPD Programme of Action.
- **Rio+20** (2012); precedents Rio+5, Rio+10.
- **Copenhagen+30** (Social Summit, 2025 — Second World Summit for Social Development, Doha).
- **NPT Review Conference**: every 5 years since 1970 — set by Article VIII.3 of the treaty itself.

These reviews are constituted as either **special sessions of the GA**, **regional review conferences**, or **functional commission review themes**.

### 2.10 Peacebuilding Architecture Review (PBAR)

- **Five-year cycle** since 2005 (when the PBC was created by twin GA/SC resolutions 60/180 and 1645).
- 2025 PBAR called for by **A/RES/75/201 and S/RES/2558 (2020)** — fourth review.
- 2024 informal phase; 2025 formal phase; twin GA/SC outcome resolutions (Nov 2025) with 31 operative paragraphs.

### 2.11 ECOSOC functional commissions

- Bureau two-year terms align with **review and policy cycles** under ECOSOC resolutions **2010/10** and **2012/7**.
- Functional commissions adopt **multi-year programmes of work** with thematic review.
- CSW (46 members, four-year terms per ECOSOC dec. 2002/210); CSocD; CND; CCPCJ; CSTD; CPD; Statistical Commission; UNFF.

### 2.12 Pact for the Future (2024) follow-up architecture

- Adopted as **A/RES/79/1** on 22 September 2024 at the Summit of the Future.
- Two annexes: **Global Digital Compact** and **Declaration on Future Generations**.
- **Review architecture:**
  - **Global Digital Compact** — high-level review in **2027**.
  - **Declaration on Future Generations** — high-level plenary meeting during GA sessions; first review.
  - **Comprehensive review of the Pact** — at start of **83rd GA session (2028)**.
- 2025 implementation overseen by USG Guy Ryder; PGA Philemon Yang convened three informal interactive dialogues (March-July 2025) including a July dialogue specifically on the 2028 review structure.

### 2.13 GA revitalization: biennialization, triennialization, clustering, sunset clauses

Standing GA practice — referenced repeatedly in revitalization resolutions adopted by the **Ad Hoc Working Group on Revitalization of the Work of the General Assembly**:

- **Biennialization** — moving an agenda item to a two-year cadence.
- **Triennialization** — three-year cadence.
- **Clustering** — grouping related items.
- **Elimination** — removing items entirely.
- **Sunset clauses** — automatic expiration absent affirmative renewal.

The GA Rules of Procedure (Annex VIII) addresses agenda item management. Despite years of advocacy, **only minor progress** has been achieved on systematic application — a key concern that UN80 MIR seeks to address.

## 3. Standard Review-Related Language Patterns (for NLP/grep work)

These are the recurring formulae that flag a "review element" inside UN mandate texts. They cluster in the **operative paragraphs**.

### 3.1 Closing-clause / continuing-engagement language

- **"Decides to remain seized of the matter"** — near-universal final operative paragraph of SC resolutions; preserves SC primacy over GA (Charter Art. 12).
- **"Decides to remain actively seized of the matter"** — emphasized variant.
- **"Decides to consider the question further at its [n]th session"** — GA equivalent.
- **"Decides to keep the situation under review"** — both organs.

### 3.2 Time-bound review / sunset triggers

- **"Decides to review the implementation of the present resolution at its [n]th session"**
- **"Decides that the mandate shall be reviewed [no later than / by / before] [date]"**
- **"Decides that the [measures/sanctions] shall be reviewed every twelve months"**
- **"Expresses its intention to review the [mandate / measures] [no later than / by] [date]"**
- **"Decides to extend the mandate of [mission/panel] until [date]"**
- **"Calls upon … to report back to [the Council/Assembly] [no later than / by] [date] for review"**

### 3.3 Reporting requests (the bulk of review hooks)

- **"Requests the Secretary-General to report"** to the [Council/Assembly] [at its … session / every six months / annually / by … date].
- **"Requests the Secretary-General to submit a comprehensive report"**
- **"Requests the Secretary-General to keep the [Council/Assembly] regularly informed"**
- **"Requests the Secretary-General to provide an oral briefing every 90 days"**
- **"Requests an interim report and a final report"** (typical for Panels of Experts).

### 3.4 Comprehensive / strategic review language

- **"Calls for a comprehensive review of [the mandate/architecture]"**
- **"Requests the Secretary-General to commission an independent strategic review"**
- **"Decides to convene a high-level review"**
- **"Stresses the importance of regular review"**
- **"Notes the need to review … on a periodic basis"**

### 3.5 Lifecycle / lifecycle-decision verbs (from UN80 MIR resolution)

The "five-verb formula" from the March 2026 landmark resolution:

> **renewal, adaptation, merger, replacement or retirement**

These verbs are the canonical taxonomy of mandate-lifecycle decisions going forward.

### 3.6 Periodic / cycle language

- **"… on a quadrennial basis"** / **"every four years"** (QCPR).
- **"every 4.5 years"** (UPR cycle).
- **"every five years"** (NPT RevCon, PBAR, conference +5 reviews).
- **"annually"** / **"every twelve months"** / **"every six months"** / **"every 90 days"**.
- **"on a biennial basis"** / **"biennialization"**.
- **"on a triennial basis"** / **"triennialization"**.

### 3.7 Sunset / termination

- **"unless the [Council/Assembly] decides otherwise"**
- **"shall expire on [date] unless renewed"**
- **"the mandate shall terminate upon [condition]"**
- **"shall remain in force until reviewed by the [Council/Assembly]"**

### 3.8 PPBME / programme planning terms

- **"strategic framework"**, **"programme plan"**, **"programme budget"**, **"proposed programme budget"**;
- **"monitoring of implementation"**, **"methods of evaluation"**;
- **"results-based budgeting"** / **"results-based management"**;
- **"expected accomplishments"**, **"indicators of achievement"**, **"performance measures"**.

## 4. Cross-Cutting Observations

1. **Two parallel languages of "review":** an explicit operative-paragraph language ("decides to review …") and an implicit cyclical practice (treaty bodies, UPR, QCPR, HLPF, conference +N reviews) that is anchored in foundational resolutions rather than re-stated each time.

2. **86% of active mandates lack any review/sunset language** (UN80 finding) — meaning the majority of work-creating decisions in the UN system have no built-in expiry or review trigger. The March 2026 resolution is the first system-wide attempt to require these clauses going forward.

3. **The mandate-budget disconnect** is the most persistent operational problem identified in both the 2006 and 2025 reviews: mandate-creating organs (GA plenary, SC, HRC, ECOSOC) sit institutionally separately from the budget-deciding organ (Fifth Committee/ACABQ), and the cycle lengths do not align.

4. **For PPB 2026/27 analysis:** review/renewal language inside individual mandates is the most direct lever for budgetary impact. A mandate without a sunset/review clause is, in practice, perpetual — and the corresponding programme line is structurally hard to reduce.

5. **"Inactive, duplicative, or fully implemented mandates"** (the March 2026 resolution's specific category) is the canonical first-pass filter for reduction work, with a December 2026 deadline.
